Section 01
What the Pilot Is
01

The Jurassic English Pilot Programme is a structured institutional evaluation framework — not a sample lesson, not a free trial, and not an open-ended classroom experiment.

The pilot creates a controlled evidence base around a defined learner group, named implementation roles, agreed decision gates, and a final rollout recommendation. It is built for procurement, academic leadership, and investor-grade proof of adoption readiness.

Four Elements Every Pilot Contains

Element What it means
Scoped Cohort A specific learner group, class level, or programme segment agreed before launch. Not the whole school. Not a general impression.
Implementation Plan A practical delivery plan aligned to the institution's timetable, teacher capacity, and standards context.
Teacher Preparation Orientation and delivery guidance before classroom use begins, so the evidence produced is meaningful and replicable.
Evidence Collection Structured capture across student work, teacher fidelity, progression fit, and operational load throughout the pilot window.

What the Pilot Is Not

Not a free trial
The pilot is a commercial engagement with defined scope, named roles, and a final recommendation. It requires a real institutional commitment and produces a real deliverable.
Not an open-ended impression
Pilot outcomes are measured against agreed criteria, not general enthusiasm. The final report reflects structured evidence, not subjective teacher feedback alone.
Not a replacement for scoping
The pilot begins after institutional readiness is confirmed. If an institution is not yet ready, we recommend a smaller entry point — an Audit Sprint or advisory session — first.
Not a one-size decision
Three commercial pathways (Advisory-First, Institutional MVP, Scale Readiness) mean the pilot is scoped to fit the institution's actual readiness, not a fixed template.

Section 02
Who the Pilot Is For
02

The pilot is most useful when a buyer has moved beyond general interest and needs a structured way to test fit, feasibility, and implementation risk before committing to full licensing or multi-class deployment.

School Leaders
You need board-ready evidence before approving a new curriculum pathway.
Use the pilot to evaluate whether Jurassic English supports your standards, academic positioning, teacher capacity, and parent-facing curriculum story.
Academic Directors
You need to know whether the methodology holds up in live classrooms.
Use the pilot to assess teacher readiness, lesson fidelity, progression logic, and the quality of student reasoning evidence your classes can produce.
Centre Owners
You need a low-risk way to differentiate before investing in full rollout.
Use the pilot to test commercial fit, operational load, teacher adoption, and whether the programme creates a defensible market position.

Qualifying Criteria

An institution is ready for a pilot when they can confirm all of the following:

Criterion Why it matters
Named Pilot Sponsor A senior decision-maker who can protect the pilot mandate and receive the final recommendation.
Defined Learner Group A specific cohort that can be dedicated to the pilot. Without this, evidence cannot be attributed reliably.
Protected Timeline A 6–8 week window that does not conflict with exams, major events, or teacher absences.
Teacher Champion At least one teacher who is willing and available for the orientation and implementation phases.
Commercial Intent A genuine adoption decision — not research, not general curiosity. The pilot is a procurement tool, not a demonstration.

If your institution does not yet meet all qualifying criteria, we will recommend a more appropriate entry point — such as an Audit Sprint or advisory session — before moving to a pilot.

Section 03
The 4-Phase Structure
03

Four phases. Four decision gates. One adoption recommendation.

Every pilot follows the same operating logic: prepare the institution, activate classroom delivery, measure implementation evidence, then decide the correct commercial pathway. Exact scope depends on institution size and readiness.

01
Setup
Scope and Readiness Review
Define the institutional problem, pilot boundary, and success criteria before any classroom activity begins.
  • Pilot scope note
  • Readiness checklist
  • Success measures agreed
Gate: Proceed only if the institution can name accountable roles, protect the pilot window, and supply baseline context.
02
Week 1
Teacher and Admin Orientation
Prepare the delivery team to implement the methodology consistently enough for evidence to be meaningful.
  • Orientation record
  • Delivery protocol
  • Evidence capture plan
Gate: Proceed only if the teacher team can demonstrate delivery readiness and the sponsor accepts the evidence protocol.
03
Weeks 2–7
Guided Classroom Implementation
Run the pilot in live classrooms while measuring academic value, teacher fidelity, and operational feasibility.
  • Evidence log
  • Teacher fidelity notes
  • Operational risk register
Gate: Continue, narrow, or pause if evidence quality, teacher capacity, or operational load falls outside agreed threshold.
04
Week 8
Pilot Review and Rollout Recommendation
Convert pilot evidence into a clear institutional decision and commercially realistic next step.
  • Pilot summary
  • KPI block
  • Rollout recommendation
Gate: Select next pathway: advisory continuation, institutional MVP, scale readiness, revised pilot, or no rollout.

Section 04
What Gets Measured · Deliverables · Governance
04

What Gets Measured

The pilot is judged by evidence, not enthusiasm. Each pilot produces a practical evidence base that helps institutional buyers decide whether Jurassic English should move into a larger implementation pathway.

🧠
Student Reasoning Evidence
Comprehension, evidence use, justification, discussion, and written response quality
📋
Teacher Fidelity
Consistency of Thinking Cycle delivery, questioning routines, and feedback expectations
📈
Progression Fit
Level match, language demand, text complexity, and assessment alignment
⚙️
Operational Load
Scheduling, preparation time, administrator oversight, and parent communication
🏛️
Commercial Readiness
Whether the programme supports a credible full rollout or licensing pathway

What Institutions Receive

  • Pilot scope and readiness note
  • Teacher orientation and delivery protocol
  • Evidence capture framework
  • Weekly review notes and risk register
  • Student reasoning evidence sample review
  • Teacher fidelity and implementation observations
  • Operational load assessment
  • Final pilot report with rollout recommendation

Governance Roles

Pilot Sponsor
Senior institutional owner who protects the pilot mandate, confirms commercial intent, and receives the final recommendation.
Academic Lead
Curriculum or academic decision-maker responsible for standards fit, learner placement, and interpretation of evidence.
Implementation Lead
Operational owner who coordinates timetable, materials access, communications, and pilot administration.
Teacher Champion
Classroom practitioner who models adoption, surfaces delivery friction, and supports teacher feedback quality.

Commercial Pathways and Next Step
05

Three pilot pathways for different levels of readiness. The commercial model keeps adoption low-risk while preserving seriousness.

Each pathway is scoped before launch and designed to produce an evidence-backed decision, not an open-ended trial.

Entry Level
Advisory-First Pilot
Best for institutions still clarifying curriculum problem, standards pressure, or internal readiness.
Consultative pilot design, materials review, leadership advisory, and limited implementation planning before classroom activation.
Most Common
Core Pathway
Institutional MVP Pilot
Best for schools or centres ready to test one learner group, one level, or one defined programme segment.
A contained classroom pilot with teacher orientation, evidence capture, review cadence, and final rollout recommendation.
Scale Track
Scale Readiness Pilot
Best for multi-class, multi-campus, or school-group buyers evaluating a wider adoption pathway.
Pilot architecture focused on governance, teacher consistency, operational load, and hub-and-spoke expansion planning.
